\section{Starting Point}
\label{sec:work-report}

Despite decades of awareness and policy activity, society still lacks a reliable, comparable, and uncertainty-quantified estimate of how much \emph{floating  macrolitter (i.e., anthropogenic or mostly plastic pieces with diameter $<$ 5\,mm)} is transported by major European rivers \citep{gonzalez2021floating,weiss2021missing,oswald2026}. Even for the river Rhine that is among the best-instrumented river systems worldwide, recent monitoring research have revealed striking inconsistency in abundances as well as \textbf{unaccounted-for-litter}: a 16-month interception study near Cologne estimates an annual macrolitter transport of roughly \(\sim\)3{,}000--4{,}700\, tonnes per year (tpy) towards the North Sea \citep{gnann2026river}, whereas downstream observational estimates for floating plastic transport in the Rhine--Meuse delta near Rotterdam are far lower in the order of \(\sim\)65\, tpy), with strong variability driven by hydrology \citep{van2022hydrology}. While part of this difference can arise from retention, entrapment, vertical mixing, (temporary) storage, and fragmentation, its magnitude points to a central bottleneck: \emph{data uncertainty} caused by heterogeneous methods, limited temporal coverage, and monitoring technologies that are difficult to scale and harmonize across sites  \citep{Gallitelli2024,smith2025insights,oswald2026}. 

Crucially, the next generation of monitoring strategies (e.g., continuous RGB camera streams, airborne spectroscopy, and high-revisit satellite imagery) does not necessarily solve this bottleneck by itself: it shifts the limiting factor toward \emph{data analysis}, because the volume and complexity of big data imagery makes manual interpretation challenging. Addressing the river Rhine discrepancy therefore requires \emph{artificial intelligence-native monitoring pipelines} that transform raw imagery into comparable indicators with verifiable and traceable uncertainties.

This uncertainty matters because rivers are widely recognized as a major pathway transporting mismanaged plastic waste from land into aquatic and marine environments\sg{ \citep{gesamp1987,perham1987,unep2021,galgani2024}}, yet reported global inputs still span large ranges. Global synthesis studies estimate that rivers deliver on the order of \(\sim\)1--3\,million tonnes of plastic per year to the ocean, with emissions concentrated in a subset of river systems \citep{lebreton2017river,meijer2021more}. At the same time, scenario studies and initiatives project that without effective action, annual plastic emissions to aquatic systems could grow substantially over the coming decades \citep{pew2020breaking,unsecretariat2025worldenvironmentday,deeney2026global}. Translating these global narratives into actionable mitigation requires sustainable, long-term, and spatially resolved observations at the river-basin scale---precisely where today’s monitoring strategies remain weakest and where automated, scalable analyses become indispensable.

The Rhine basin is an ideal methodological testbed for closing this monitoring and unaccounted-for-litter abundance gap. First, it combines high societal relevance with comparatively low macrolitter loads, meaning that any methodology robust enough for the Rhine is likely transferable to more polluted systems where detection is often easier. Second, the basin already hosts active monitoring infrastructure, including continuous interception and classification efforts supported by citizen scientists \cite{gnann2026river} and long-duration transport studies in the Rhine–Meuse delta \cite{van2022hydrology}. Third, the Rhine is embedded in a relatively dense Earth-observation ecosystem enabling a principled investigation of how to integrate observations from ground, air, and space—provided that robust learning-based methods exist to fuse, calibrate, and quality-control heterogeneous multi-source data streams.

However, existing monitoring approaches remain fragmented across spatio-temporal scales, in part because the analysis burden grows faster than sensing capability. On the ground, standard monitoring still relies heavily on manual visual counting and localized campaigns, which are resource-intensive (e.g., observers) and difficult to sustain at high frequency over many sites. Bridge-mounted cameras combined with deep learning can automate detection and yield continuous time series \citep{vanlieshout2020automated}, and our own collaborative work demonstrates object-detection pipelines under realistic river conditions and confounders \citepPIRusswurm{hagenbeek5841770exploring}. Yet converting detections into comparable flux estimates remains uncertainty-dominated \citep{vriend2025uncertainties}, motivating harmonized workflows \citep{vanemmerik2023harmonized} and, critically, AI methods that propagate uncertainty from detection to flux and basin-scale indicators \citep{cottom2024local}.

From an Earth-observation perspective, remote sensing offers the unique opportunity to extend monitoring beyond isolated sites and campaigns, but it faces two persistent challenges: (i) detectability against water and complex backgrounds and (ii) consistent ground validation. Diagnostic optical features of plastic-based objects commonly found in riverine as well as ocean-harvested litter have been well documented using spectroscopic technologies covering the visible to shortwave infrared spectrum, providing the physical basis for multi-/hyperspectral sensing approaches \citep{garaba2018airborne,garaba2020uvswir,goddijn2018concept,garaba2021concentration,knaeps2021hyperspectral,de2023hyperspectral,giz2023}. Recent work further shows that riverine litter can be detected and quantified from very fine pixel multispectral satellite imagery with attention to sensor characteristics and transferability across sites \citep{garaba2024riverine,smith2025insights}. However, turning these opportunities into operational monitoring requires sensor-aware AI pipelines that handle rare events, domain shift, and cross-sensor fusion, anchored in validated spectral reference data and uncertainty-aware calibration.

The ambitious \textbf{PLASTRACK} aims to address this frontier by developing disruptive fine-tuned methods for integrated multi-scale monitoring of riverine macrolitter in the Rhine basin, combining (1) RGB imagery from fixed bridge cameras and ship-mounted cameras, with (2) airborne multi- and hyperspectral optical sensing (e.g., MAIA), and (3) satellite multi- and hyperspectral data streams (e.g., Sentinel-2, PlanetScope, EMIT, PRISMA, EnMAP). A central premise of PLASTRACK is that these sensing modalities only become a coherent monitoring system through AI: scalable detection and classification models, cross-sensor calibration informed by optical evidence, and uncertainty propagation that translates heterogeneous imagery into comparable basin-scale indicators and flux estimates.

Longstanding contributors (i.e., co-authored articles, collaboration in research projects) will provide in-kind infrastructure that strengthens feasibility and benchmarking: the Plastic Litter Project (PLP, Lesbos) contributes established protocols and multi-sensor field experience for controlled validation campaigns \citep{topouzelis2019detection,papageorgiou2022sentinel2}, and Wageningen University (van Emmerik) contributes operational river-plastic monitoring know-how, harmonization concepts, and training opportunities through existing camera-based workflows \citep{vanlieshout2020automated,vanemmerik2023harmonized}.}

\paragraph{Ground sampling campaigns and validation.} Field campaigns (e.g., controlled, mesocosm, in-situ experiments) remain the gold standard to best characterize detectability limits, establish spectral reference libraries, and validate sensing end-products for floating litter \citep{martinezvicente2019measuring,vighi2022monitoring, giz2023,Gallitelli2024}). The Plastic Litter Project (PLP) exemplifies the controlled experiment approach by deploying artificial targets and synchronizing in-situ, UAV, and Sentinel-2 acquisitions to benchmark separability of plastics from other floating materials \citep{topouzelis2019detection,papageorgiou2022sentinel2}. Follow-up work quantified marginal detectability thresholds across sensors and target sizes, demonstrating that observability depends strongly on sub-pixel abundance and environmental conditions \citep{garaba2020uvswir,garaba2024riverine,papageorgiou2022sentinel2,biermann2020finding}. Already, detecting, classifying, and quantifying riverine litter garbage patches has been demonstrated through dedicated discrete experiments leveraging visual inspection combined with multispectral imaging sensors, for example fixed on bridges \citep{kataoka2020macrodebris,vanlieshout2020automated,degiglio2021wej} and drones \citep{geraeds2019uav,wolf2020aplasticq,cortesi2022uavmultispectral,valenzuela2026mapping}.
An long-standing open methodological gap for rivers is to translate campaign-derived observability envelopes into \emph{sensor-aware} processing chains that generalize across geographic sites and seasons; this requires spectral reference data, finer pixel resolution imagery from airborne platforms, and validation protocols that connect optical signatures to what multi-/hyperspectral sensors can reliably observe  \citep{giz2023,hu2025logic}. These validated reference points are also essential for training and benchmarking AI models under rare-event conditions and changing backgrounds, enabling scalable monitoring products across large EO archives \citep{russwurm2023large}. This is consistent with recent near-field monitoring software that quantifies floating macroplastic transport from river-surface imagery using deep learning and in-situ validation \citep{kataoka2020macrodebris}. 

\paragraph{Bridge and proximal imaging for continuous flux estimates.}
Bridge-based camera monitoring has matured into an operationally attractive approach for high-frequency observations at strategic cross-sections. Deep-learning-based detection from fixed cameras has been demonstrated as a scalable alternative to manual counting, enabling continuous time series of floating macrolitter occurrence \citep{vanlieshout2020automated}. However, recent analyses stress that observational uncertainty, site-specific visibility, and protocol choices can dominate errors when converting detections and counts into fluxes \citep{vriend2025uncertainties}, motivating harmonized workflows to translate heterogeneous camera campaigns into comparable estimates across rivers and time \citep{vanemmerik2023harmonized}. The key frontier is to make proximal streams transferable and uncertainty-calibrated and to connect them quantitatively to EO-based screening; recent work demonstrates how object detection can be grounded in realistic river conditions and confounders (e.g., vegetation and complex backgrounds), providing a basis for robust deployment and cross-site generalization (Fig.~\ref{fig:plastichyacinths}) \citepPIRusswurm{hagenbeek5841770exploring}.
The inconsistency gap is our motivation with the aim to bridge intercomparisons including the unaccounted for litter \citep{blettler2026macroplastic}

\paragraph{Earth observation and imaging spectroscopy for synoptic monitoring.}
Satellite remote sensing provides the unique opportunity to extend monitoring beyond isolated geographic sites, despite persistent observation challenges. A primary challenge in detection and definitive identification of targets such as plastics is the presence of heterogeneous optically active objects and materials in natural aquatic environments, which can create spectral ambiguities in multispectral imagery \citep{giz2023,garaba2024riverine,hu2025logic,samuriwo2024comparative,marye2025remote}. 
Typical detection of anomalies can be achieved using multispectral data but the identification step of having higher confidence in the target material does require hyperspectral information to mitigate spectral ambiguities or very fine pixel resolution for object based approaches. Additionally, the widely reported lack of in-situ observations continues to hinder validation of reported studies or proposed approaches in literature \citep{van2020physical,bansal2025efficient,cerra2024detection,wang2026detecting}.
Progress has been made in mapping the distribution of riverine litter patches using high spatial resolution imagery (e.g., PlanetScope at ~3m/pixel, Sentinel-2 at ~10m/pixel, Skysat 0.5 m/pixel) from a range of multispectral satellite observations \citep{garaba2024riverine,mohsen2023machine,smith2025insights}. The present suite of multispectral satellites (e.g., PlanetScope ~1 day, Sentinel-2 ~5 days,) when leveraged in synergy have the capability of offering multiscale imaging but at a cost for the commercial missions offering high revisit of sub-daily intervals (i.e., PlanetScope, Skysat). 
Furthermore, the present demonstrated mapping capabilities have been limited to assumed presence of plastics as large fraction of riverine litter being detected as anomalies appearing in imagery that tend to be inferred from positive spectral-based index values (e.g., Plastic Index, Floating Debris Index) or machine learning classification approaches. The effects of heterogenous background influenced by varying levels of optically active constituents of riverine systems (e.g., coloured dissolved organic matter, algae, suspended solids) have been explored and mitigation workflow proposed utilizing band anomaly analyses of neighboring pixels \cite{garaba2024riverine}.
One additional research gap has been advancing on the definite identification of plastics or improved distinguishing of materials in riverine litter from satellite imagery has yet to be explored using current hyperspectral spaceborne technologies (e.g., EMIT, EnMAP, PRISMA) with the support of coincident airborne surveys for validation and in-situ sampling for expanding on spectral reference library (see section Deployed catchments as instrumented observatories.). Hyperspectral technologies covering the visible to shortwave infrared spectrum have long been demonstrated to be highly relevant in the accurate identification of natural and anthropogenic materials in all environments \citep{cloutis1989spectral,ohall6138760plastic,de2023hyperspectral,kuhn2004hydrocarbon,knaeps2021hyperspectral,goddijn2018concept}. Converting these capabilities into operational basin-scale products requires AI pipelines that handle rare events and domain shift and that fuse EO indicators with proximal and in-situ measurements; scalable learning-based monitoring has been shown to turn large EO archives into repeatable, spatially explicit products for riverine litter hotspot screening \citepPIRusswurm{russwurm2023large,perez2025scalable}.

Interception-based systems provide two critical functions: physical capture for composition and mass conversion, and continuous time series that are otherwise rare in large rivers. A prominent example is the long-term Rhine monitoring near Cologne, combining interception with classification and enabling annual macrolitter transport estimates and source attribution \citep{gnann2026river}. Deployed cleanup technologies increasingly pair capture with diagnostics, but peer-reviewed evidence remains fragmented and often complemented by technical reporting \citep{oceancleanup2026smartriversurvey}. Conceptually, the field is shifting from one-off device demonstrations to treating interception sites as instrumented observatories that can anchor calibration and validation for camera- and EO-based methods, including uncertainty propagation from detections to flux and mass-balance reasoning. In an AI-native monitoring stack, such observatories provide the reference points needed to calibrate cross-sensor models and to reconcile synoptic EO indicators with local flux measurements.

\subsection{Objectives}

\todo{First draft- to be refined with the concrete work packages in mind.}

\textit{Overarching aim:} To advance the fundamental scientific and methodological foundations required for robust, basin-scale monitoring of riverine \textbf{macro-litter} patches from multi-sensor observations.\\

\textit{Central hypothesis:} Basin-scale macroplastic monitoring can become scientifically reliable when treated as a \textbf{closed-loop multi-modal monitoring problem} that couples (i) principled detectability theory, (ii) physically grounded calibration, and (iii) uncertainty-aware multi-scale data fusion.

\begin{description}
    \item[H1 Detectability is predictable.]
    Whether macroplastics can be detected is mainly determined by a small set of measurable factors: sensor properties (resolution, spectral bands, noise, viewing geometry) and environmental conditions (background, turbidity/foam, illumination and season). These factors can be summarized as \emph{detectability envelopes} that generalize across sites and acquisition settings.

    \item[H2 Calibration makes estimates comparable and transferable.]
    Experiments with known targets and matched in-situ reference data can reliably translate observations into quantitative macroplastic indicators (e.g., counts, covered area, size proxies) and quantify systematic biases from preprocessing, atmosphere/water effects, and domain shift, enabling transfer across sensors and campaigns.

    \item[H3 Fusion enables basin-scale inference with uncertainty.]
    By combining continuous local observations (bridge/ship imagery) with episodic airborne/satellite snapshots in a probabilistic fusion framework, we can infer basin-scale hotspot persistence and event-driven variability, while propagating uncertainty from detection and calibration to downstream hotspot and flux indicators.
\end{description}

\paragraph{WP1 (Bonn): Ground and UAV data foundation + benchmarks for macroplastic detection (H2/H3).}
\textbf{Goal.} Build a reproducible, research-grade dataset and evaluation protocol from bridge- and ship-based imagery, complemented by targeted UAV acquisitions over hotspots, to support calibration and fusion method development (without establishing an operational monitoring service).\\

\paragraph{WP2 (Bonn): Learning-based detection and upscaling from point observations (H2/H3).}
\textbf{Goal.} Develop robust AI methods that (a) detect and quantify macroplastic indicators in ground imagery, and (b) upscale sparse observations to spatial patterns using learned location/context encodings and uncertainty estimates.\\

\paragraph{WP3 (UOL): Detectability envelopes and sensor-aware calibration for imaging spectroscopy (H1/H2).}
\textbf{Goal.} Establish a sensor- and environment-aware understanding of macroplastic detectability, grounded in laboratory hyperspectral analyses and observation geometry, and translate it into practical calibration guidance.\\

\textbf{Goal.} Develop methods to connect airborne hyperspectral mapping with satellite observations, and to fuse episodic remote-sensing snapshots with continuous ground observations into basin-scale hotspot inference with quantified uncertainty.\\


\subsection{Handling of research data}

2.4.1 Data description: Expected PLASTRACK data (retrieved or to be measured) will be from remote sensing (e.g., relative reflectance, absorption, attenuation, scattering coefficients), water quality (e.g., temperature, salinity, chlorophyll-a, suspended particulate matter, coloured dissolved organic matter, Secchi disk depth) and the associated metadata available via online portals (e.g., Copernicus Browser, NASA Earthdata, Global Plastic Hub) or data repositories (e.g., PANGAEA, SeaBASS). Essential water quality and related environmental variables stated above will be generated or gathered through data mining in the project. Field data will be recorded on datasheets to be digitized as ASCII tab delimited files or Excel sheets. File names and column headers will contain vital metadata. Images will be stored, processed in the cloud or local workstations whilst back-up will be done using external hard drives with password protection.
2.4.2 Documentation and data quality: Raw and derived data products will be organized, named, keyworded, labelled, versioned, and stored, as well as formatted following standards (e.g., SeaDataNet, Ocean Best Practices System). Methods to generate relevant metadata will be based on prior peer-reviewed Data Description publications, e.g. (Garaba et al., 2020; Garaba and Dierssen, 2020; de Vries et al., 2023; Garaba et al., 2023). PLASTRACK published datasets in open-access will have unique persistent identifiers as previously done by co-PI Garaba . Any developed algorithms used to handle the data will be made open-source. Data organization will be done using various software (e.g., Notepad++, MS Excel).
2.4.3 Storage and technical archiving the project: UOL: The Information Technology department provides secured cloud storage. All data will be stored in PLASTRACK secure cloud folders and a standalone backup hard drive. Satellite imagery will be retrieved from space agency open-access repositories or via secure ftp from collaborators for datasets-of-opportunity sharing.
2.4.4 Legal obligations and conditions: All research material will have a CC BY 4.0 licence immediately after data curation or at the same time as a peer-reviewed Data Description article is published. European General Data Protection Regulations will be applied whenever relevant.
2.4.5 Data exchange and long-term data accessibility: Data publication will be in long term (>10 years) online open-access repositories (e.g., PANGAEA, SeaBASS). Data exchange will also benefit from (4.12.1 Outreach, dissemination and transfer of knowledge).
2.4.6 Responsibilities and resources: All PLASTRACK team members (co-PIs as coordinators) will have the responsibility for data handling, quality assurance, quality control and curation consistent with open-science and FAIR policies. PLASTRACK deliverables will also be made open-access.
